CASE STUDY: SOUDAN IRON MINE, Soudan, MN

The Soudan Underground Mine (SUM) near Soudan, MN is a prime example of where biology and geology have interacted in the Minnesota's past. The SUM contained economically viable iron ore with an abundance of nearly pure iron-ore. Mining of iron began at this site in 1882, first as an open pit mine, then as an underground mine. The SUM operated in Northern Minnesota from 1901 until mining operations ceased in 1962, when competition from the much cheaper taconite mining industry put them out of business because it was too expensive to be profitable1. Currently the mine exists as part of Soudan Underground state park in Northern Minnesota and provides a rare opportunity to study a Banded Iron Formation (BIF), associated aquifer water chemistry, and the life forms that live deep underground that seep out through the boreholes.

In abandoned mine shafts, the SUM system contains signs of modern microbial life2.These signs hint at a complex story of microbes and fluid as an important biogeochemical system relating to the ~2.7 Ga rocks. A series of boreholes located on the 27th level continue to vent water. Microorganisms are visible growing near these boreholes and along the surface of the water in several locations. These microbes can provide a window into the biology of the early Earth.
