How was the Universe Created?

13.5 Billion years ago: The Big Bang!

The universe began with a massive explosion that generated space and time, along with all of the matter and energy in the universe. It is unknown what caused this explosion, but it may have been inflation. At this point in time, there were no particles such as protons, neutrons, bosons, neutrinos, or electrons. This means that atoms did not exist because there was nothing to make them out of.

Less than a second after the Big Bang

As the universe continues to expand, it starts to cool. Basic forces, such as gravity, are now in existence. The universe now contains the fundamental particles of matter and energy: quarks, electrons, photons, neutrinos and others. At the end of this era protons and neutrons form, but atoms are still unable to form.

3 seconds after the Big Bang

The universe is now cool enough for the formation of three simple elements: hydrogen, helium and lithium. No other elements are able to form.

300,000 years after the Big Bang

As expansion continues, electrons link up with hydrogen and helium nuclei to make atoms. No other elements can be made at this time. Elements heavier than lithium, but lighter than iron are made inside stars. All other elements are made in supernova explosions.

300 Million Years after the Big Bang

The first stars and galaxies are formed from the existing elements, hydrogen and helium. Scientists are still unsure which came first. These stars create new elements in their cores and create even more elements when they undergo supernova explosions.

about 5 billion years before now

The solar system begins to form. It started as a "nebula", or cloud of gas and dust. It contained mostly hydrogen and helium, but also a small amount of heavier atoms which were created by a supernova explosion. The nebula started to collapse in on itself and was drawn together due to gravity. The particles of dust and gas inside the nebula smashed into each other and created larger and larger particles. The nebula continued to collpase and started to rotate. The majority of the nebula's mass was drawn to the center of the cloud.

As gravity continued to pull matter into the center of the disk, the density and pressure became great enough for nuclear fusion to begin. The sun was born!

As the outer parts of the disk started cooling off, matter started to condense and clump together. Eventually, as more and more matter clumped together, larger clumps called planetesimals formed. Planetesimals continued to clump together until protoplanets formed and then protoplanets collided and formed the planets. Rocky, or terrestrial, planets formed closer to the Sun and gas planets formed farther away from the sun. This happened because solar wind blew most of the lighter elements, such as hydrogen and helium, into the outer solar system, leaving behind heavier elements and even some organic molecules for the terrestrial planets to form out of.
