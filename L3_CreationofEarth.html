Lesson 3: WHAT WAS EARLY EARTH LIKE?

We already learned about how Earth formed earlier in the semester, but, now, we are going to see how Earth's formation contributed to the creation of life on Earth.

about 4.6 Billion Years Ago

The Earth coalesces from the solar nebula at the same time that the Sun and other planets formed. The Earth was very, very hot at this time, hot enough that it was molten. Shortly after the Earth's formation, a large planetismal, Theia, struck the Earth. Some of the debris from this impact ended up in orbit around the Earth and combined to form the moon. This impact also gave the Earth its tilt.

Earliest Evidence of First Life

It can be difficult to know exactly when life started because there is not much evidence of early Earth in the rock record because it was still molten with no crust early on. The earliest evidence for life was potentially found in a zircon from 4.4 billion years ago in Jack Hills, Australia1. A "zircon" is a mineral containing zirconium, silicon, and oxygen. The geochemistry and mineral inclusions seem to indicate that the zircon was formed from a hydrous, sediment-driven magma1.

We can examine carbon isotopes for evidence of biologic activity. Carbon-12 is more likely to be used in biologic activity than Carbon-13 because it is lighter. So a high ratio of carbon-12/carbon-13 is characteristic of biogenic carbon. A zircon from 4.1 billion years ago was rich in the carbon-12 isotope and may be evidence that there was life 4.1 billion years ago1.

What are microbes?

"Microbes" is a general term applied to the bacteria and archea domains2.

Microbes represent 50% of the biomass carbon

Microbes exist anywhere nutrients, water and energy are found: within glaciers and basalt fractures, in undersea thermal vents, and in acidic waters, to name a few2.

Microbes are critical to many processes that affect water quality, climate and agriculture2.

Microbes exert control on their local environments by shaping their geochemical surroundings through their metabolic and growth needs2.
