CASE STUDY: SOUDAN IRON MINE, Soudan, MN

The Soudan Underground Mine (SUM) near Soudan, MN is a prime example of where biology and geology have interacted in the Minnesota's past. The SUM contained economically viable iron ore with an abundance of nearly pure iron-ore. Mining of iron began at this site in 1882, first as an open pit mine, then as an underground mine. The SUM operated in Northern Minnesota from 1901 until mining operations ceased in 1962, when competition from the much cheaper taconite mining industry put them out of business because it was too expensive to be profitable1. Currently the mine exists as part of Soudan Underground state park in Northern Minnesota and provides a rare opportunity to study a Banded Iron Formation (BIF), associated aquifer water chemistry, and the life forms that live deep underground that seep out through the boreholes.

In abandoned mine shafts, the SUM system contains signs of modern microbial life2.These signs hint at a complex story of microbes and fluid as an important biogeochemical system relating to the ~2.7 Ga rocks. A series of boreholes located on the 27th level continue to vent water. Microorganisms are visible growing near these boreholes and along the surface of the water in several locations. These microbes can provide a window into the biology of the early Earth. Geology at the Soudan Iron Mine

Photo of the banded iron formation found in the Soudan Underground Mine. Credit: Lindsey Meuwissen

Banded Iron Formations are sedimentary rocks composed of alternating bands of iron-rich and silica-rich mineral layers. Minnesota was the bottom of an ocean around 2.7 Ga. In that deep water setting, subaqueous volcanoes erupted and drove the circulation of high temperature fluids through thick stacks of volcanic rocks. At high temperatures, this circulation also drove the leaching of elements, Hot fluids (~35o degrees celsius)3 that were released from the Earthâ€™s mantle through hydrothermal vents leached out many elements such as ferrous iron and H4SiO4 from the host volcanic rocks and into a solution. By upward convection, the hydrothermal fluids eventually hit cool layers of ocean water. The high thermal gradient causes elements in solution to precipitate onto the seafloor due to conductive cooling, resulting in the formation of the Si-Fe-rich chemical sedimentary layers. When the solution comes into contact with cooler seawater, the H4SiO4 precipitates out of it. For Fe2+ in solution, with the exception of solutions of a high acidity, as soon as the solution comes into contact with oxygen, Fe3+ will be created from the Fe2+. The hydrothermal circulation occuring during the Archean, allowed for a large reservoir of Soluble Iron(II) (Fe2+) to build up in the oceans. The iron rich layer of BIFs formed when the Fe(II) was oxidized to Fe(III), causing it to precipitate into the sediment. There are three main ways iron could have been oxidized: (1) oxidation catalyzed by bursts of UV radiation, (2) oxidation when Fe(II) contacts pockets of oxygenated waters made by early oxygenic photosynthesizers, or (3) direct oxidation by iron-oxidizing microorganisms. The iron formation at Soudan is possibly a record of microbial evolution occurring at the time.
